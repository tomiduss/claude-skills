# Brand Strategy Frameworks

Reference material for the Brand Director to use during the discovery interview
and for the Design System Architect when writing brand guidelines.

---

## Brand Positioning Statement

Use this template to crystalize brand positioning:

```
For [target audience]
who [statement of need or opportunity],
[brand name] is a [category]
that [statement of key benefit].
Unlike [competitive alternative],
[brand name] [statement of primary differentiation].
```

**Example:**
For freelance designers who struggle to present work professionally,
Folio is a portfolio platform that makes your work look as good as it is.
Unlike Squarespace or Cargo, Folio is built specifically for design portfolios
with intelligent layout that adapts to your work.

## Brand Archetypes

Choose one primary archetype (and optionally one secondary) to anchor the
brand personality. Each archetype implies a communication style, visual
language, and emotional territory.

| Archetype | Core Drive | Voice | Visual Cues | Examples |
|-----------|-----------|-------|-------------|----------|
| **Innocent** | Optimism, simplicity | Warm, honest, uplifting | Soft colors, organic shapes, light | Dove, Coca-Cola |
| **Sage** | Knowledge, truth | Authoritative, clear, thoughtful | Clean layouts, structured type, muted palettes | Google, PBS |
| **Explorer** | Freedom, discovery | Bold, adventurous, independent | Earthy tones, expansive imagery, rugged type | Patagonia, Jeep |
| **Outlaw** | Revolution, disruption | Provocative, raw, unapologetic | Dark palettes, edgy type, high contrast | Harley-Davidson, Diesel |
| **Magician** | Transformation, vision | Inspiring, imaginative, confident | Rich colors, dynamic motion, premium feel | Apple, Disney |
| **Hero** | Mastery, courage | Direct, empowering, action-oriented | Bold type, strong contrast, geometric | Nike, FedEx |
| **Lover** | Intimacy, beauty | Sensual, elegant, indulgent | Warm tones, flowing forms, rich textures | Chanel, Godiva |
| **Jester** | Joy, play | Witty, irreverent, fun | Bright colors, playful type, unexpected layouts | Old Spice, Ben & Jerry's |
| **Everyman** | Belonging, community | Friendly, approachable, relatable | Warm neutrals, rounded shapes, accessible type | IKEA, Levi's |
| **Caregiver** | Service, compassion | Gentle, reassuring, supportive | Soft palette, open layouts, warm imagery | Johnson & Johnson |

| **Creator** | Innovation, imagination | Expressive, inventive, inspiring | Bold palettes, creative layouts, distinctive type | LEGO, Adobe |

## Brand Personality Dimensions

Pick 3-5 adjectives that describe the brand's character. Draw from different
categories to create a distinctive blend:

**Sincerity:** down-to-earth, honest, wholesome, cheerful, friendly, genuine
**Excitement:** daring, spirited, imaginative, up-to-date, energetic, bold
**Competence:** reliable, intelligent, successful, focused, efficient, precise
**Sophistication:** upper-class, charming, smooth, elegant, refined, luxurious
**Ruggedness:** outdoorsy, tough, strong, athletic, resilient, raw

A brand that's "intelligent, daring, and charming" feels very different from
one that's "honest, reliable, and friendly" — even in the same industry.

intentionally.

## Typography Personality

Typeface selection communicates personality before anyone reads a word.

| Category | Personality | Best For |
|----------|------------|----------|
| **Geometric sans** (Futura, Avenir, Poppins) | Modern, clean, tech-forward | SaaS, fintech, startups |
| **Humanist sans** (Gill Sans, Fira Sans, Source Sans) | Friendly, approachable, clear | Consumer apps, education |
| **Neo-grotesque** (Helvetica, Univers, Suisse) | Neutral, professional, universal | Corporate, enterprise |
| **Transitional serif** (Georgia, Merriweather, Lora) | Trustworthy, established, editorial | Finance, media, publishing |
| **Modern serif** (Didot, Playfair Display, Cormorant) | Elegant, high-fashion, premium | Luxury, beauty, lifestyle |
| **Slab serif** (Rockwell, Roboto Slab, Zilla Slab) | Bold, grounded, industrial | Hardware, manufacturing, sports |
| **Monospace** (JetBrains Mono, Fira Code, Space Mono) | Technical, precise, developer-facing | Dev tools, code, engineering |
| **Display / decorative** (varies widely) | Distinctive, memorable, characterful | Headlines, logos, hero sections |

**Pairing principles:**
- Contrast, not conflict: pair a distinctive display font with a clean body font
- Same designer/foundry pairings often harmonize naturally
- Limit to 2 families (3 maximum) per brand
- Weight contrast matters as much as family contrast

## Brand Guidelines Structure

When producing the final brand guidelines document, follow this structure:

1. **Brand Story** — purpose, vision, mission, values, personality
2. **Positioning** — statement, audience, archetype, differentiation
3. **Logo** — primary usage, variations, clear space, minimum size, don'ts
4. **Color Palette** — full specs (HEX, RGB, HSL), semantic usage, accessibility
5. **Typography** — families, scale, hierarchy, pairing rules
6. **Voice & Tone** — characteristics, context variations, vocabulary
7. **Imagery** — photography/illustration style, icon style, guidelines
8. **Graphic Elements** — patterns, textures, decorative elements
9. **Applications** — web, mobile, social, email, print
10. **Do's and Don'ts** — quick-reference rules, common mistakes

## Naming Strategies

If the brand needs a name, consider these approaches:

| Strategy | Description | Examples |
|----------|------------|----------|
| **Descriptive** | Says what you do | PayPal, Salesforce, Airbnb |
| **Invented** | Made-up word | Google, Kodak, Spotify |
| **Metaphorical** | Symbolic meaning | Amazon, Nike, Slack |
| **Acronym** | Initials | IBM, HBO, AWS |
| **Founder** | Personal connection | Tesla, Disney, Bloomberg |
| **Compound** | Combined words | Facebook, Instagram, YouTube |
| **Altered spelling** | Unique twist | Flickr, Lyft, Fiverr |

A good brand name is: short, pronounceable, memorable, available (domain + socials),
and doesn't mean something unintended in other languages.
